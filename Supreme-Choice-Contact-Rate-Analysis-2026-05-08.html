What we know — five findings

1
The break is between March and April. That's the date to investigate.

Blended contact rate held above 50% in Feb 2026 (55.8%) and March 2026 (51.9%). In April it dropped to 23.6%, and the early-May trajectory points lower again. There is no gradual decline — there's a clear inflection at the start of April. Whatever changed (staffing, dialer config, lead routing, hours of operation, speed-to-lead workflow), it happened around that turn of the month. That's the conversation to have.

2
Lead quality didn't drop — quote-of-contacted held steady.

Of the leads that did get contacted, the rate that turned into a quote was 68.3% in March and 67.6% in April. Same conversion strength once the conversation happens. This rules out 'the leads got worse' as the explanation. Whatever the issue is, it's about reaching the leads, not what happens after.

3
Volume nearly doubled the same month contact rate halved.

March delivered 79 leads. April delivered 144 — a +82% jump. Tier 2 leads tripled (25 → 75). Home SearchB doubled (11 → 23). When lead volume jumps faster than contact capacity, contact rate is the first thing to compress. Worth checking whether dialer hours, headcount, or callback workflow scaled with the volume.

4
Tier 2 is your most resilient source — anchor on it.

Tier 2 contact rate dropped less than the others (54.7% → 25.9%, a -28.7 pt drop) despite delivering the largest volume increase (+62% MoM). Compare to Tier 1, which fell 71 points (84% → 12%). Tier 2 should be the priority source while we work the recovery — it's holding.

5
The Q1 playbook works — let's document it before it fades.

From November 2025 through March 2026, the account ran a five-month stretch above the 50% blended contact rate mark. That's not theoretical — that's a real, recent benchmark. Capturing what was different in that period (volume range, working hours, who was on the phones, response times) gives us a concrete recovery target rather than a directional one.

Recommended next steps

  1. Pinpoint what changed at the end of March. Walk through it on the call: was there a staffing change, a dialer or CRM update, a workflow shift, a vacation, a new lead-routing rule? The date stamp is so clean that the cause should surface quickly once we ask.
  2. Check capacity vs. volume in April. Tier 2 lead volume tripled and Home SearchB doubled. Confirm the call team had bandwidth to keep pace — if not, we may need to pace the inflow until contact capacity catches up, or expand the contact window.
  3. Defend Tier 2 first. It dropped less than any other source. Hold its bid stance and let it stay the workhorse while we figure out the rest.
  4. Document the Jan–Mar process. While the team still remembers what they were doing. Hours, scripts, speed-to-lead, who-was-on-shift. That's our recovery target.
  5. Re-measure in two weeks. If the partial-month contact rate isn't back above 25% by end of May, that's our signal to pull spend on the weakest sources rather than continue paying for leads we're not reaching.
Methodology. Home campaign only. Contact Rate = Contacted ÷ Leads, calculated per source per month from GOAL platform exports. Last 3 months focus = Mar / Apr / May 2026. May 2026 is partial (8 days at time of report). Months with strong inbound calls can produce contact-rate values above 100% in the underlying data; those are capped at 100% on charts for readability. Sources with fewer than 3 leads in a month are excluded from per-source charts.
