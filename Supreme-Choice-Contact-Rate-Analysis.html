Volume Doesn't Predict Contact Rate

The most surprising correlation in the data: high-volume sources don't necessarily reach customers more often. In fact, on Home CA the relationship is mildly inverse.

Read this chart like a quadrant: top-right = a source that gives you both volume and contact reach (no Home sources fully sit there). Top-left = small but high-quality (Tier 3, Home SearchB used to live here). Bottom-right = high volume but you can't reach them (where Tier 2 sits today).

Three Patterns Worth Acting On

Pattern 1

Auto's Premium Paid Search sources are the best contact channels in the whole account

PPS 2B, 4, and 6 don't just give us leads — they generate inbound calls. Inbound callers are self-selecting: they've already decided to talk. Contact rates effectively above 100% reflect this. These three sources are responsible for almost all of the 25 calls in the last 60 days.

Implication: When we activate Auto next week, these are the multipliers we keep aggressive. The "contact rate problem" you've been hearing about doesn't exist on these sources at all — the problem is that we're barely participating in their auctions yet (only $74 of spend across all three combined).

Pattern 2

Home SearchB is the reach champion on Home — and trending up sharply

Home SearchB went from 0% contact rate (3/18 window) to 11.8% (4/16) to 27.3% (4/29 MTD), and 48.5% across the longer 60-day combined window. That's the most reliable trajectory in the entire campaign.

Implication: The recommendation we already made — push Home SearchB from 170% to 185% to land above 2nd-position market — is doubly justified by this contact-rate trend, not just the Quote CPA. A source that already reaches customers half the time is exactly where we want more lead flow.

Pattern 3

Tier 1 contact rate is stuck at 17% — strongest signal yet for the publisher-drift theory

Tier 1 has the largest opportunity pool by far (45,000+ auctions in 60 days), and its contact rate has been flat at 17–18% for three consecutive 30-day windows while the rest of the campaign has improved. A flat curve on the largest source means a small bad publisher is dragging the average down — exactly the pattern you described from the 2025 lead drought.

Implication: The advertiser drift audit we queued up for this week now has three independent data signals pointing at the same root cause: low win rate at the position floor (0.43%), high lead volume but flat conversion downstream, and now flat contact rate while the rest of the campaign improves. We should expect the audit to find 1–3 publisher IDs underneath Tier 1 that need to come off the list.

What Drives Contact Rate — Cross-Cutting Signals

We checked four candidate explanations against the source-level data. Here's what holds up:

HypothesisHolds Up?Evidence
Higher bids → higher contact rate No Home Search (highest bid at $8.40) has lower CR than Home SearchB ($4.90). Cheaper sources actually reach customers better.
Better ad position → higher contact rate Partially Home SearchB (pos 4.91, best CR 48.5%) and Tier 1 (pos 6.14, lowest non-thin CR) line up. But Home Search at pos 5.25 underperforms its position.
Higher click-through rate → higher contact rate No (slightly inverse) Tier 2 has the highest CTR (45%) but the lowest CR (25.8%). Easy clicks may indicate looser targeting and harder-to-reach prospects.
Inbound calls baked into the source → higher CR Yes The three call-driven Auto sources are the only channels in the account with CR > 100%. The call inversion of "you call them" → "they call you" is the strongest single driver.

The strongest correlation in the data isn't with bidding mechanics at all — it's with whether the source produces inbound calls vs outbound dials. That has implications well beyond bid optimization.
